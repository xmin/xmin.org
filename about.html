What is Christian Minimalism?

The disinterest [...] is a disinterest in doing it again, not in it as it is being done by those who developed the last advanced versions. &mdash Donald Judd, “Specific Objects”

I find the heart of Christianity tantalizing but elusive. I can describe to you moments when I think I’ve glimpsed it, but the news is stale quickly. Who needs more words to read? And yet it is something. It’s not nothing. And I find it equally stale to pretend that it is for me to invent anew every moment. I find myself wanting some structure even while recognizing that no structure is absolute.

Christian Minimalism, then, is my attempt to distill a minimalist form of Christian structure, so that I can get on with my life. It specifies three daily practices for use by me, and any other individual who wishes to pick them up off the Internet floor.

The first practice is to read the Gospels. The age of taboos, of censorship, is over. XMin doesn’t tell me what not to read or think or do. And yet, there can be a focal point to my view of human culture, a center. For the Christian Minimalist, this center point is the four Gospels, taken together: Matthew, Mark, Luke and John.

The second practice is to pray with intentions. This is where I try to align my will, my intentions, with the will of God. This feels to me to be too intimate to talk about, but I trust that if you’re reading this, then you’ll get out of it what you’re “supposed” to. There, that was an intention, for example. All types of prayer fall under this heading: meditation, adoration, confession, thanksgiving, supplication, intercession, etc. There’s no reason for it not to include every conscious thought I have, in theory. Ok, I’m blushing.

The third practice is where the rubber meets the road: love one another. The standard is Christ’s love for his disciples, primarily in the washing of their feet (this is in John 13), and ultimately in the Crucifixion. For me this is very mundane. It means not losing patience with my children. It means being sensitive to my wife. It means serving my coworkers. Reading the Gospels and praying with intentions find their fulfillment in loving one another. And if you only do this one practice, you could rightly call yourself a minimalist Christian Minimalist.

So that is where I’m coming from with Christian Minimalism. If you’d like to follow along as I explore these themes, then follow me on Twitter or friend me on Facebook.

Chad Whitacre
