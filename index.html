Christian Minimalism

By Chad Whitacre

Christian Minimalism is a theological experiment. To what extent can catholic Christianity be reconstituted on the basis of the atomic individual? From experience and culture, we get the idea that the individual is absolute, inviolable. The individual can be crushed, but never broken. The individual is the only truly indivisible unit, culturally speaking. The individual is the building block of culture.

We also get the idea that the individual is the fundamental unit of divine communication. Every prophecy begins as an impression on the screen of consciousness. Only with the passage of time and the playing out of these ideas over millenia do they assume the mantle of prophecy, of divine communication. This process is as yet underway.

There are veins of these ideas in the Bible:

  • Adam being made in the "image of God" is parallel to Seth being made in the "image of Adam:" a child, a peer-in-waiting. Compare the genealogy in Luke, where God is the pater familias.
  • Abraham bargains with God.
  • Jacob wrestles God to a draw.
  • Jeremiah foretells the day when each individual will know God equally.
  • The Canaanite Woman rises to Christ's challenge to beat him at his own game.
  • Peter invites us to become "partakers of the divine nature."

Christian Minimalism asks the question, can catholic Christianity be built atop the absolute individual? I take catholic Christianity to be more or less the definition of Rome, with some allowance for Orthodox, Anglican, Protestant, and Anabaptist distinctives. What becomes of these systems when one begins with the individual person as absolute?

I see several tasks:

  • Define the individual as atom from cultural sources.
  • Draw out the individualist vein in the Bible. Attempt to make the case that the trajectory of scripture and history is towards the atomic individual.
  • Define the group on the basis of the atomic individual.
  • Give an account of the possibility of divine communication.
  • Define and evaluate specifically the catholic system on the basis of the atomic individual.

What, we ask, does Christianity mean in an age of individualism?
