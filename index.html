Christian Minimalism

By Chad Whitacre

Christian Minimalism is a theological movement. It intends to be continuous with the mainstream current of Christianity through the ages, and simultaneously to appreciate the beauty of God's work in the life of every person, Christian or not. It is minimalist in that it addresses the interpersonal level only, entrusting structural-level definitions to other entities. Its goal is to encourage the human family towards Godliness.

The thrust of Christian Minimalism is Love, agape, at the interpersonal level. Using the Bible as our first source, the external focus out of which culture properly radiates (and all of culture is available, on the table; there are no taboos, that age is over), we attempt to capture in words what we all know in our hearts. Our hearts are the internal focus complementing the external.

Christian Minimalism is a subset of systematic theology based on the individual human person. It is a thoroughly individualistic account of Christianity. At first blush, this seems at odds with the Christian mainstream, which rejects individualism. Christian Minimalism's gambit is that, scripturally and from general experience, the case can be made for a system, recognizably Christian, built upon the atomic individual.

So the Christian Minimalist atom is the person, the atomic free will. Free enough for our purposes, mind you, not for God's. We need not defend a concept of free will absolute enough to satisfy the human demands of divine judgement. It is enough that our experience of will be free—that of our own and of that of others. The thesis I wish to defend is that the moral choice of the individual is absolute. In the final analysis, the pronouncement of the Pope carries no more weight at the interpersonal level than that of the pauper. The Pope, qua Pope (that is, as the Pope, rather than Joseph Ratzinger, mortal man) is a person playing a role in a system. Joseph Ratzinger lying awake in his bed at night is a man, red-blooded. Pope Benedict is something else. Joseph Stalin understood this when he said to his son, "You're not Stalin and I'm not Stalin. Stalin is Soviet power. Stalin is what he is in the newspapers and the portraits, not you, no not even me!"

The work remains to be done (my intention is to build The XMin Bible in order to do the necessary research), but my preliminary thesis is that Jesus addresses most directly the interpersonal level. He is talking to villagers, provincial bumpkins, telling them how to relate to their neighors, as in, the people they can spit at and hit. Jesus addresses the structural level only indirectly, I suggest.

And that, dear friends, is a start.
